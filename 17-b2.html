The Reflex Arc: GCSE Biology Guide

What is a Reflex Arc?

A reflex arc is the neural pathway that an electrical impulse follows during a rapid, automatic response to a stimulus (a reflex action). This process is vital for survival as it helps to minimise damage to the body, for example, by quickly pulling your hand away from something hot.

Crucially, most reflex actions do not involve the conscious part of the brain in the initial response, making them much quicker than voluntary actions.

Key Components and Pathway

The reflex arc involves specific components working in sequence. You need to know the order of these parts and their functions.

  • Stimulus: An external change (e.g., heat, pressure, light) detected by the body.
  • Receptor: Specialised cells (e.g., pain receptors in the skin) that detect the stimulus and convert it into an electrical impulse.
  • Sensory Neuron: Transmits the electrical impulse from the receptor to the Central Nervous System (CNS - the spinal cord in this case).
  • Relay Neuron (Interneuron): Found within the CNS (spinal cord). It links the sensory neuron to the motor neuron via a synapse.
  • Motor Neuron: Carries the electrical impulse from the CNS to the effector organ.
  • Effector: A muscle or gland that receives the impulse and carries out the response (e.g., a muscle contracting to move a limb).
  • Response: The resulting automatic action (e.g., pulling your hand away).

The Role of Synapses

Neurones do not physically touch; there are tiny gaps between them called synapses.

  • When an electrical impulse reaches the end of a neuron, it triggers the release of chemical messengers called neurotransmitters.
  • These chemicals diffuse across the synaptic gap and bind to receptors on the next neuron, causing a new electrical impulse to be generated.
  • In a typical reflex arc, there is a synapse between the sensory and relay neuron, and another between the relay and motor neuron.
