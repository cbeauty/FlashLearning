Genetic Variation for GCSE Students

What is Variation?

Variation refers to the differences between individuals within a species or between different species. These differences are crucial for survival and evolution. Variation can be inherited (due to genes) or acquired (due to the environment), or a combination of both.

Sources of Genetic Variation

Genetic variation primarily comes from three key sources:

  • Mutations: These are permanent changes in the DNA base sequence. While some have no effect, others can lead to changes in proteins and thus the phenotype (observable characteristics).
  • Meiosis: This type of cell division, which produces gametes (sex cells), shuffles alleles into new combinations in a process called crossing over and independent assortment.
  • Sexual Reproduction: The fusion of male and female gametes from different parents creates a unique combination of alleles in the offspring, leading to genetic diversity.

Key Terms to Remember

  • DNA: Deoxyribonucleic acid, a complex polymer that contains the genetic instructions for development and function.
  • Chromosome: Thread-like structures of DNA found in the nucleus, carrying genetic information in the form of genes.
  • Gene: A small section of DNA on a chromosome that codes for a specific protein.
  • Allele: Different versions of the same gene (e.g., the gene for eye colour has alleles for blue, brown, etc.).
  • Genotype: The combination of alleles an organism has for a particular gene (e.g., Ff or FF).
  • Phenotype: The observable characteristics of an organism, determined by its genotype and environment.
