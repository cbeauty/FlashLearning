The Central Nervous System (CNS)

Your guide for GCSE Biology revision

What is the Nervous System?

The nervous system allows humans to react to their surroundings and to coordinate their behaviour. It's a complex network of cells called neurones that carry information as electrical impulses.

The Role of the CNS

The nervous system has two main parts. The **Central Nervous System (CNS)** is the command centre.

  • The CNS consists of the **brain** and the **spinal cord**.
  • It receives information from receptors (specialised cells that detect stimuli).
  • The CNS then coordinates a response and sends impulses to effectors (muscles or glands).
Pathway of a Nervous Impulse:
Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory Neurone → CNS (Coordinator: Relay Neurone) → Motor Neurone → Effector → Response

Neurones and Synapses

Nerves are bundles of neurones. Different types of neurones have specific roles:

  • Sensory neurones carry impulses from receptors to the CNS.
  • Relay neurones are found within the CNS and link sensory and motor neurones.
  • Motor neurones carry impulses from the CNS to effectors.

There is a tiny gap between two neurones called a synapse. The electrical impulse is converted into a chemical message (neurotransmitter) to cross this gap, then back to an electrical impulse in the next neurone.

Reflex Actions

Reflex actions are automatic, rapid responses that are important for protecting the body from danger. They bypass the conscious part of the brain to save time. The pathway involved is called a reflex arc.

Example: If you touch something hot, receptors in your skin send an impulse to the spinal cord (CNS). A relay neurone quickly passes the impulse to a motor neurone, telling your arm muscle (effector) to contract and pull your hand away, all before you consciously register the heat.
