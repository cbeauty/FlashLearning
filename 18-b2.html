GCSE Biology: The Human Brain

Introduction to the Brain

The brain is a complex organ made of billions of interconnected neurons. It is the control centre for the nervous system, coordinating all our complex behaviours and bodily functions. Different regions of the brain are responsible for specific functions.

Key Parts and Their Functions

GCSE specifications typically focus on these main parts and their roles:

Cerebral Cortex (Cerebrum)

The highly folded outer layer of the brain, split into two hemispheres. It is responsible for higher-order processes such as:

  • Intelligence and memory
  • Consciousness and personality
  • Language and conscious thought
  • Initiating voluntary movement

Cerebellum

Located underneath the back of the cerebral cortex. Its main functions are:

  • Coordination of muscle activity
  • Balance and posture

Medulla Oblongata (Medulla)

Found at the base of the brain where it connects to the spinal cord. It controls vital **unconscious activities**, such as:

  • Heart rate
  • Breathing rate
  • Reflexes like coughing, sneezing, and swallowing

Hypothalamus

Located in the centre of the brain, this small area is crucial for **homeostasis**. It regulates:

  • Body temperature
  • Water balance and thirst
  • Hunger and fullness
It also produces hormones that control the pituitary gland.

Pituitary Gland

Often called the "master gland". It is attached to the hypothalamus and produces a wide range of hormones that control other glands and bodily processes, including growth and metabolism.

Studying the Brain

Neuroscientists use various methods to study the brain, including studying patients with brain damage, electrical stimulation, and modern scanning techniques like MRI. The complexity and delicacy of the brain make studying and treating disorders very challenging.
