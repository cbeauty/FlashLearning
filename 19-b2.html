Your guide to undifferentiated cells in biology

What are Stem Cells?

A stem cell is an undifferentiated cell of an organism that has the ability to divide to form many more cells of the same type. From these, other cells can arise through the process of differentiation (specialisation).

In simple terms, they are "blank" cells that can become specialised cells like muscle cells, nerve cells, or blood cells.

Types of Stem Cells

Embryonic Stem Cells (Animals)

Found in early embryos, these cells can differentiate into most different types of human cells. Scientists can grow them in a lab and encourage them to form new tissues for medical treatment.

Adult Stem Cells (Animals)

Found in limited numbers in certain locations in the body, such as bone marrow. They can only differentiate into a limited range of cell types, primarily blood cells.

Meristem Cells (Plants)

Plants have stem cells in regions called meristems (found in the tips of shoots and roots). These cells can differentiate into any type of plant cell throughout the life of the plant.

Uses of Stem Cells in Medicine

Stem cell technology offers potential treatments for various diseases and injuries. They could be used to replace damaged cells or grow new organs.

  • Treating conditions such as diabetes and paralysis.
  • In therapeutic cloning, an embryo is produced with the same genes as the patient, so the stem cells from it would not be rejected by the patient's body.
  • Plant meristems can be used to quickly and economically clone rare species or disease-resistant crops.

Ethical and Social Issues

The use of stem cells, especially embryonic stem cells, involves important ethical considerations:

  • Potential risks, such as the transfer of viral infections.
  • Some people have strong ethical or religious objections to the use of embryos in medical research, as they believe life begins at conception.
  • Therapeutic cloning raises questions about the creation and destruction of embryos for treatment purposes.
