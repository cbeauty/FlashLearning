The Synapse

What is a Synapse?

A synapse is a small gap or junction between two neurones (nerve cells), or between a neurone and an effector cell like a muscle or gland. Electrical impulses cannot jump this gap, so the signal is transmitted using chemical messengers.

How Does it Work? (Synaptic Transmission)

Nerve impulse transmission across a synapse involves several steps:

  • An electrical impulse reaches the end of the first neurone (presynaptic neurone).
  • This triggers the release of chemical neurotransmitters from vesicles.
  • Neurotransmitters cross the gap (synaptic cleft).
  • They bind to receptors on the second neurone (postsynaptic neurone).
  • This binding stimulates a new electrical impulse in the second neurone.
  • Neurotransmitters are then quickly removed to stop the stimulation.

Why are Synapses Important?

Synapses are vital because:

  • They ensure nerve impulses travel in one direction.
  • They allow the brain to process information for complex functions.
  • They are key in rapid reflex actions.
