Introduction to White Blood Cells

White blood cells, also known as leukocytes, are a crucial part of your body's immune system. They circulate in your bloodstream and tissues to detect and destroy invading pathogens, such as bacteria, viruses, and fungi.

  • They are made in the bone marrow.
  • Unlike red blood cells, they have a nucleus.
  • There are two main types covered in GCSE Biology: Phagocytes and Lymphocytes.

Phagocytes

Function: Engulfing Pathogens

Phagocytes are responsible for phagocytosis, the process of engulfing and digesting unwanted microorganisms.

  • They have a sensitive cell surface membrane that detects chemicals produced by pathogens.
  • Once they encounter a pathogen, they surround and ingest it into their cytoplasm.
  • Digestive enzymes within the phagocyte then break down and destroy the pathogen.
  • They are easily recognised by their multi-lobed nucleus.

Lymphocytes

Function: Producing Antibodies and Antitoxins

Lymphocytes play a more specific role in the immune response by recognising specific antigens on the surface of pathogens.

  • They produce antibodies, which are special proteins that bind to the antigens on pathogens. This helps in several ways:
    • Makes it easier for phagocytes to spot and engulf the pathogens.
    • Can cause pathogens to clump together.
    • Can cause pathogens to burst open.
  • They also produce antitoxins to neutralise harmful toxins released by some pathogens.
  • Some lymphocytes become memory cells, providing long-term immunity against specific pathogens.
  • They have a large, round nucleus that takes up most of the cell.
