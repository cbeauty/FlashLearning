Alleles and Inheritance (GCSE Biology)

Your guide to understanding genetic variation

What are Alleles?

In simple terms, alleles are the different **versions of a gene**. For example, the gene for eye colour has different alleles, such as the allele for brown eyes and the allele for blue eyes.

You inherit one allele for each gene from your mother and one from your father. The combination of these two alleles determines your characteristics.

Key Definitions

  • Gene: A small section of DNA on a chromosome that codes for a specific protein or characteristic.
  • Allele: Different forms or versions of the same gene.
  • Genotype: The combination of alleles an organism has for a particular gene (e.g., BB, Bb, or bb).
  • Phenotype: The physical characteristic that is expressed, determined by the genotype and environment (e.g., brown eyes).
  • Homozygous: When an organism has two identical alleles for a gene (e.g., BB or bb). "Homo" means "same".
  • Heterozygous: When an organism has two different alleles for a gene (e.g., Bb). "Hetero" means "different".

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

Alleles can be dominant or recessive, which affects how they are expressed in the phenotype.

  • Dominant Allele: Always expressed in the phenotype, even if only one copy is present. Represented by a **capital letter** (e.g., **B** for brown eyes).
  • Recessive Allele: Only expressed in the phenotype if two copies are present (i.e., no dominant allele is present). Represented by a **lowercase letter** (e.g., **b** for blue eyes).

For example:

  • **BB:** Homozygous dominant -> Brown eyes (dominant allele is present)
  • **Bb:** Heterozygous -> Brown eyes (dominant allele masks the recessive one)
  • **bb:** Homozygous recessive -> Blue eyes (only recessive alleles are present)
