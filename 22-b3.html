Understanding Genotypes: A GCSE Guide

Welcome, GCSE students! This page will help you understand the core concepts of genotypes and how they relate to the characteristics you see in living organisms.

What is a Genotype?

A genotype is the combination of alleles an organism has for a particular gene. It's the genetic code for a specific trait, often represented by letters like Bb, BB, or bb.

Key Terms

  • Gene: A section of DNA coding for a protein or characteristic.
  • Allele: A different version of a gene.
  • Dominant Allele: Always expressed in the phenotype, even with one copy (e.g., B).
  • Recessive Allele: Only expressed if two copies are present (e.g., b).
  • Phenotype: Observable characteristics resulting from genotype and environment.

Homozygous vs Heterozygous

Alleles determine if an organism is homozygous or heterozygous:

Homozygous: Both alleles for a gene are the same. Examples: BB (homozygous dominant) or bb (homozygous recessive).

Heterozygous: Two different alleles for a gene (e.g., Bb), with the dominant allele expressed.

Example

Using the PTC tasting gene (T for taster, t for non-taster):

Genotype Description Phenotype (what you observe)
TT Homozygous dominant Taster
Tt Heterozygous Taster
tt Homozygous recessive Non-taster
