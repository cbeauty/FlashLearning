Pathogens & Communicable Diseases

Your guide to the microorganisms that cause disease, for GCSE Biology students.

What are Pathogens?

Pathogens are microorganisms that cause infectious (communicable) diseases in animals and plants. They depend on a host to survive and reproduce, often producing toxins or damaging tissues, which makes us feel ill.

Types of Pathogen

There are four main types of pathogens you need to know about:

Bacteria

  • Small prokaryotic cells that reproduce rapidly by binary fission.
  • They often cause disease by producing toxins that damage cells and tissues.
  • Examples: *Salmonella* (food poisoning), *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (TB), *Vibrio cholerae* (cholera).

Viruses

  • Not considered living organisms as they cannot reproduce on their own; they are parasitic.
  • They take over host cells, insert their genetic material, and use the cell's machinery to make more viruses, eventually causing the cell to burst.
  • Examples: HIV, Influenza, Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV) in plants.

Fungi

  • Can be single-celled or multicellular (forming threads called hyphae).
  • They produce spores which allow them to spread easily.
  • Examples: Athlete's foot in animals, Black rose spot in plants.

Protists

  • A diverse group of mostly single-celled eukaryotic organisms, many of which are parasites.
  • Examples: *Plasmodium* species which causes malaria (spread by mosquitoes as a vector).

How are Pathogens Spread?

Pathogens can be transmitted in several ways, including:

  • Air: Inhaling tiny droplets containing pathogens (e.g., common cold, flu).
  • Water/Food: Ingesting contaminated food or water (e.g., cholera).
  • Direct Contact: Transfer via body fluids or touching contaminated surfaces (e.g., HIV, athlete's foot).
  • Vectors: Carried by an organism (like a mosquito for malaria).
