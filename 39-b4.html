GCSE Biology: Clones & Cloning

What is a Clone?

A clone is a living thing that is an exact genetic copy of another organism. This means they have the same DNA.

Cloning can happen naturally through asexual reproduction (e.g., bacteria dividing), or it can be done artificially in a lab.

Cloning in Plants

Plants are relatively easy to clone. Two common methods used in horticulture and biology are:

1. Taking Cuttings

Gardeners often take a small part of a plant (a cutting) and plant it in damp soil or rooting powder, which contains hormones to encourage root growth. This produces a new plant that is genetically identical to the parent plant.

2. Tissue Culture (Micropropagation)

This method is used to produce many identical plants quickly, often for commercial purposes or to preserve rare species. It involves:

  1. Obtaining very small pieces of plant tissue, called explants.
  2. Growing the explants in a sterile agar jelly containing nutrients and plant hormones.
  3. The explants grow into small plantlets, which can then be transferred to potting trays to grow into full plants, all clones of the original parent.

Cloning in Animals

Animal cloning is more complex and involves different techniques:

1. Embryo Transplants

This method produces several identical offspring from desirable parent animals (e.g., high-quality livestock). The steps are:

  • Egg and sperm cells from high-quality parents are used for artificial fertilisation.
  • The resulting embryo is allowed to develop into a ball of cells.
  • The embryo is split into several identical smaller embryos before the cells specialise.
  • These cloned embryos are then implanted into different host (surrogate) mothers.
  • The resulting offspring are all genetically identical to each other (but share genes from both original parents).

2. Adult Cell Cloning (Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer)

This technique creates a clone of an already existing adult animal. Dolly the sheep was the first mammal cloned this way.

  1. The nucleus is removed from an unfertilised egg cell (enucleated).
  2. A nucleus is taken from an adult body cell (e.g., a skin cell) of the animal to be cloned.
  3. The adult nucleus is inserted into the empty egg cell.
  4. An electric shock stimulates the egg to divide and form an embryo.
  5. The embryo is implanted into a surrogate mother to develop until birth.
  6. The offspring is a clone of the animal that donated the adult body cell nucleus.

Benefits & Risks of Cloning

Benefits Risks / Concerns
Quickly produce many plants/animals with desired characteristics. Reduced genetic variation makes populations vulnerable to new diseases.
Preserve endangered species. Cloned animals may have health problems and a shorter lifespan.
Therapeutic cloning could produce stem cells to treat diseases (e.g., grow new tissues/organs). Ethical concerns, particularly around the potential for human cloning.
