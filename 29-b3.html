The Eukaryotic Cell

A guide for GCSE Biology students

What are Eukaryotic Cells?

Eukaryotic cells are complex cells that make up all animals, plants, fungi, and protozoa. A key feature is that their genetic material (DNA) is enclosed within a **nucleus**. They are generally larger than prokaryotic cells and contain specialised structures called **organelles** which carry out specific tasks.

Key Organelles and Their Functions

Eukaryotic cells have several sub-cellular structures. Here are the main ones you need to know for GCSE:

  • Nucleus: Contains the genetic material (DNA) that controls the cell's activities and growth.
  • Cytoplasm: A jelly-like material where many of the cell's chemical reactions occur. It contains dissolved nutrients, salts, and the other organelles.
  • Cell Membrane: A partially permeable barrier that controls the movement of substances (like nutrients and waste) into and out of the cell.
  • Mitochondria: These are the sites of aerobic respiration, where most of the energy is released for the cell's functions.
  • Ribosomes: Tiny structures where protein synthesis takes place.
  • Cell Wall (Plants/Fungi/Algae): Provides structural support and protection to the cell (not found in animal cells).
  • Chloroplasts (Plants/Algae): Contain chlorophyll and are the site of photosynthesis (not found in animal or fungi cells).
  • Permanent Vacuole (Plants): A large central sac filled with cell sap, which helps maintain turgor pressure against the cell wall.

Plant vs. Animal Cells

Both plant and animal cells are eukaryotic. However, plant cells have a few extra organelles that animal cells do not:

  • A cell wall
  • Chloroplasts
  • A permanent vacuole
