GCSE Biology: Understanding the Phenotype

Welcome, GCSE students! This page will help you understand the key biological term: phenotype.

What is the Phenotype?

The phenotype refers to the observable characteristics or traits of an organism. These are the features you can literally see or measure, such as eye colour, height, or even blood type (which can be measured in a lab).

The word "pheno" comes from the Greek word for "to show" or "to appear", helping you remember that it's all about what is *shown*.

Genotype vs. Phenotype

It's important not to confuse phenotype with genotype, which is the specific combination of alleles (different versions of a gene) an organism has for a particular trait.

Think of it this way:

  • Genotype: The *genetic code* (e.g., the letters like Bb, BB, or bb).
  • Phenotype: The *result* you can see (e.g., brown eyes or blue eyes).

The Role of Genes and the Environment

A person's phenotype is determined by both their genetic makeup (genotype) and environmental factors.

Phenotype = Genotype + Environment

Examples of Environmental Influence:

  • Height: Your genes determine your potential height, but factors like your childhood diet and overall health (environmental factors) also play a significant role in how tall you actually become.
  • Skin Colour: Genes affect how much melanin you produce, but exposure to the sun causes your skin to darken (a tan), which is an environmental effect on your phenotype.
  • Flamingos: A classic example! Flamingos are naturally white. Their pink colour phenotype comes from pigments in the organisms they eat (their diet).

Common Phenotype Examples

Here are some examples of phenotypes in humans and other organisms:

  • Eye colour (blue, brown, green, etc.)
  • Hair colour and type (straight, curly)
  • Blood type (A, B, AB, O)
  • Ability to roll your tongue
  • Presence of a genetic disorder, like cystic fibrosis
  • Flower colour in pea plants (purple or white)
