Selective Breeding (Artificial Selection)

A guide for GCSE Biology students

What is Selective Breeding?

Selective breeding, also known as **artificial selection**, is when humans choose organisms (plants or animals) with desirable characteristics and breed them together to produce offspring with those same traits. This process has been used for thousands of years to improve crops and domestic animals.

The Process of Selective Breeding

The key to selective breeding is that it must be repeated over many generations to establish a "new breed" that reliably shows the chosen characteristics in all offspring.

  1. **Decide on desired characteristics:** Humans first identify which traits are important, such as high milk yield in cows or disease resistance in plants.
  2. **Select parents:** Individuals that show the desired characteristics are chosen from a mixed population to be the parents.
  3. **Breed the parents:** The chosen parents are bred together.
  4. **Select best offspring:** From the new offspring, only those that show the desired traits most strongly are selected to produce the next generation.
  5. **Repeat:** This process is repeated continuously over many generations until the desired characteristics are consistently present in all offspring.

Examples of Selective Breeding

  • **Cows:** Bred for increased meat or milk production.
  • **Crops:** Bred for disease resistance, higher yield, or better taste.
  • **Dogs:** Bred for specific temperaments (e.g., gentle nature in guide dogs) or physical features (e.g., a Poodle's low-shedding coat).
  • **Wild Brassica plant:** Selectively bred to create a wide variety of vegetables like broccoli, cabbage, and cauliflower.

Benefits and Risks

Benefits (Pros)

  • **Economic importance:** Can lead to increased yields and better quality food for a growing human population, which is economically beneficial for farmers.
  • **Desirable traits:** Allows for the development of organisms with useful traits like disease resistance or a gentle temperament.
  • **Improved food quality:** Can result in better tasting fruits or larger produce.

Risks (Cons)

  • **Reduced gene pool:** Repeated selection reduces genetic variation (gene pool), making the whole population vulnerable to new diseases or environmental changes.
  • **Inbreeding issues:** Breeding closely related individuals (inbreeding) can increase the chance of inheriting harmful genetic defects or specific diseases (e.g., hip dysplasia in some dog breeds).
  • **Ethical concerns:** Some physical problems in animals are a direct result of breeding for extreme characteristics (e.g., breathing issues in certain dog breeds).
